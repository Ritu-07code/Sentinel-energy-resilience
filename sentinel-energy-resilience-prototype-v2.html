Detection Logic

How a vessel gets flagged

Explainable, not a black box

Step 1 — Cross-reference last known AIS ping against expected transponder interval (typically every 2–6 min for tankers).

Step 2 — If signal gap exceeds 6 hours in a sanctioned or high-risk corridor, check satellite optical/SAR pass for a vessel-shaped object at the last projected position.

Step 3 — Cross-check hull against known ship-to-ship transfer zones and prior sanctioned-fleet registries.

Step 4 — Assign a risk score (0–100) and route it into the Adaptive Procurement Orchestrator so flagged capacity isn't relied upon.

Why This Matters

From Signal to Action

Supply-side shocks rarely cause fuel shortages on their own — panic buying does. In past crises, pump queues formed 3–5 days before any real supply gap.

Sentinel tracks search intent and social chatter as a leading indicator, separate from the physical supply models, so retail stock can be pre-positioned in the specific metros showing early panic signals — before queues form.

How This Agent Decides

Drawdown Logic

Inputs — live reserve level (ISPRL storage telemetry), daily refinery consumption rate (PPAC), modelled disruption duration, and confirmed alt. supply arrival dates from the Procurement Orchestrator.

Logic — draw down just enough to cover the gap until the earliest reliable alt. cargo lands, keeping a safety buffer above a 3-day critical floor.

Why phased, not lump-sum — releasing reserve gradually avoids signalling panic to markets, which itself can spike prices further.

Human gate — Sentinel proposes the schedule; release is executed only after sign-off, same as the procurement recommendations.
